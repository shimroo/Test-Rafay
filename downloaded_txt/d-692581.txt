          gave a talk at work on "dependencies" today, trying to externalise and make explicit some of the background awareness that shapes my everyday practice as a software developer. it takes time for this awareness to develop. i remember the first time someone talked to me about how gradle uses a topological sort over a graph of dependencies to determine a build order for tasks, and what a revelation that was. i remember the first time i was shown how to get intellij to scan a java project for cyclic dependencies between packages, and coming to understand why one might want to avoid them and how to unpick them if they arose.

now dags (directed acyclic graphs) are a comfortable piece of my mental furniture, but i don't know that more junior developers are often directly introduced to them - they might come up as a data structure with associated algorithms in cs courses, but is it explained that they're fundamental to the way compilers and build tooling assemble working software from many related pieces? many developers are aware at some level that transitive dependencies can balloon out into bloat (and an ever-expanding attack surface for vulnerabilities), but how many can articulate this clearly?

a large part of my practice as a principal engineer is mentoring individuals and looking out for knowledge gaps in teams. it can be hard to recover a "beginner's mind" and remember what it was like not to know (or to be only vaguely aware of) something that became second nature to you over a decade ago, but it forces you to make your implicit knowledge explicit - which also gives you opportunities to reflect on it. teaching is learning: you go back and retread old paths, and are confronted again with long-forgotten questions.

like everyone at the moment, i wonder what the impact of llms is going to be on the way developers acquire the background awareness and instincts that guide accomplished practitioners. will they help to make implicit knowledge explicit, or will they paper over gaps in knowledge and obstruct the process of learning?
